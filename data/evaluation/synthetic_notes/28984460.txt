## ASSESSMENT

1. **Acute kidney injury, unspecified** — Likely multifactorial etiology including volume depletion and hyperglycemic hyperosmolar state. Creatinine elevated acutely with corresponding rise in BUN.

2. **Hyponatremia with hypo-osmolality** — Severe hyponatremia (118 mEq/L) contributing significantly to altered mental status. Likely SIADH or euvolemic hyponatremia given clinical presentation.

3. **Type 2 diabetes mellitus with hyperglycemia** — Poorly controlled glucose at 312 mg/dL on admission; patient on insulin therapy with suboptimal glycemic control.

4. **Hypothyroidism** — Stable; TSH within normal range on current levothyroxine dosing.

5. **Sarcoidosis** — Chronic condition, appears stable without acute pulmonary decompensation.

6. **Hypercholesterolemia** — Noted on recent lab work; not acutely contributing to current presentation.

7. **Epilepsy, unspecified, not intractable** — No seizure activity documented during admission; patient remained seizure-free.

8. **Long-term insulin use** — Patient insulin-dependent for glycemic management.

## PLAN

During hospitalization, the patient received aggressive IV hydration with careful sodium repletion to address both the acute kidney injury and severe hyponatremia. Sodium levels were monitored serially to avoid overcorrection. Insulin dosing was optimized with both basal and bolus adjustments to better control hyperglycemia. The patient's mental status improved significantly as sodium normalized and glucose came under better control. By discharge, creatinine had trended down to 1.8 mg/dL, sodium was 132 mEq/L, and glucose was in the 180–220 range. No seizures occurred during hospitalization.

Patient is discharged in stable condition with instructions to follow up with nephrology regarding residual renal function, endocrinology for diabetes optimization, and primary care. Patient should continue current antiepileptic medications and thyroid replacement. Strict adherence to fluid restriction (1 L per day) is advised given hyponatremia risk. Patient counseled on signs of hyponatremia recurrence including headache, confusion, and seizure activity, with instruction to seek immediate evaluation if these occur.
